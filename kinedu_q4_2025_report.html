Executive Summary

Q4 2025 showed significant sequential improvement with revenue up 44% QoQ to $1.11M, driven primarily by recovery in December ($445K, +23% MoM). However, the company remains significantly behind budget (-46% in Q4, -44% for full year) and generated a net loss of $96K EBITDA despite the revenue uptick. Full year 2025 closed at $3.53M revenue with -$824K EBITDA and -$897K cash burn, representing substantial underperformance against a $6.34M revenue budget and +$650K EBITDA target.

What drove Q4 performance:

  • Strong December recovery boosted quarterly revenue 44% QoQ after weak Q2-Q3 performance ($771K-$785K range)
  • Marketing spend increased 31% QoQ to $562K, suggesting volume-driven growth strategy rather than efficiency gains
  • Contribution margin improved to $292K (26.3% margin) vs $152K in Q3, indicating better unit economics at higher volumes

Key risks and watch-outs:

  • Structural revenue shortfall: 44% below budget for full year suggests fundamental issues with conversion, retention, or pricing assumptions
  • Negative operating leverage: Increased marketing spend did not translate to profitability—higher volumes still produced losses

Lowlights & Structural Risks

Critical underperformance areas requiring immediate attention

  • Revenue Shortfall (44% below budget): The company generated $3.53M against a $6.34M budget, missing by $2.81M. This is not a marginal variance—it represents a fundamental disconnect between the business model assumptions and reality. The gap widened throughout the year with Q4 at -46% vs budget despite being the strongest revenue quarter. Main lever: Conversion rate or ARPU must be structurally lower than modeled. Fix requires data-driven diagnosis of whether the issue is top-of-funnel (traffic/installs), mid-funnel (free-to-paid conversion), or retention (churn).
  • Negative Operating Leverage (EBITDA -$824K vs +$650K budget): As the company scaled marketing spend and grew revenue, losses widened rather than narrowed. Q4 showed 44% revenue growth QoQ but EBITDA improved only 62% (still deeply negative at -$96K). This suggests high variable costs or that acquisition costs are not recovering within the measured period. Main lever: Unit economics (LTV:CAC ratio) appear broken. Fix requires either dramatic CAC reduction (50%+), ARPU improvement (pricing/upsells), or churn reduction to extend LTV.
  • Extreme Revenue Volatility (3.3x range Jan-Dec): Monthly revenue ranged from $103K to $445K within 2025, with a catastrophic -76% drop in February and multiple 20-30% swings. This volatility indicates the business lacks reliable, recurring revenue drivers. Main lever: Predictability requires either subscription business stabilization or consistent marketing performance. Fix requires forensic analysis of what drove February collapse and March recovery, then systematizing whatever worked in high-performing months.
  • Contribution Margin Below Viability (26% actual vs 37% budget in Q4): Even at peak Q4 performance, contribution margin was 26.3%, vs budgeted 37%. This 11pp gap compounds through the entire operation—every dollar of revenue generates 11 cents less than expected to cover fixed costs. Main lever: CAC efficiency is the primary driver. Actual CAC appears significantly higher than budgeted, or conversion rates significantly lower, producing fewer subscribers per dollar spent. Fix requires channel-by-channel CAC analysis and ruthless pruning of inefficient acquisition sources.
  • Cost Creep Despite Revenue Miss (+6.4% over budget): While revenue fell 44% short, operating costs exceeded budget by 6.4% ($1.57M actual vs $1.47M budget). This suggests insufficient cost discipline as top-line targets were missed. Main lever: SG&A spending needs immediate review. Fix requires zero-based budgeting exercise to cut non-essential costs given the revenue reality. Salaries at $1.04M (6% over budget) need scrutiny given the current burn rate.

Strategic Signals & Implications

Data-driven insights for decision-making

1 ARPU recovery is now the critical path—not CAC reduction
The KPI data reveals the core issue: ARPU collapsed from $35 (Jan) to $22-25 (Aug-Oct), a 29-38% decline that directly caused the revenue shortfall. While CAC is 28% above budget in Q4 ($1.61 vs $1.26), conversion improved dramatically (+63% vs budget), meaning the company is acquiring customers efficiently on a unit basis. The problem is each customer generates 15% less revenue than expected ($26 actual vs $30 budget ARPU). At current volumes, every $1 improvement in ARPU adds ~$350K in annual revenue with zero additional marketing spend. Root cause diagnosis required: Is ARPU down due to (a) shift from annual to monthly plans (check subscription mix), (b) promotional pricing to drive volume, (c) faster churn shortening LTV, or (d) lower-tier product mix? Fix levers: (1) limit monthly plan availability to force annual commitments, (2) implement price increases, (3) upsell existing customers to higher tiers, (4) reduce churn through retention campaigns.
2 Conversion is working—volume assumptions were wrong
Conversion rates exceeded budget by 63% (7.87% actual vs 4.82% budget in Q4), climbing steadily from 3.76% in January to 8.36% in December. Free trial conversion also performed well at 14.72% in Q4. This means the company is extremely effective at converting users who enter the funnel—the problem is not enough users reached the funnel. The 44% revenue miss despite 63% better conversion indicates top-of-funnel volume (installs, traffic) was dramatically overestimated. Strategic implication: The business model works at converting users, but either (1) marketing spend doesn't generate expected install volumes, or (2) budgeted install volumes were unrealistic. The path forward isn't optimizing conversion (already strong)—it's either scaling top-of-funnel volume cost-effectively, or accepting the TAM is smaller than modeled and right-sizing the business accordingly. Key question: What were budgeted vs actual monthly install volumes? If installs matched budget, then lower ticket/ARPU is the issue. If installs fell short, then channel strategy needs fundamental rethink.
3 December performance provides template but sustainability is unproven
December generated $445K revenue (best month of the year) with -$41K EBITDA (best month since April). If this is repeatable, the company could be on track to near-breakeven operations. However, December's strength could be seasonal (holiday gifting, New Year fitness resolutions for parents) or due to one-time marketing campaigns. The critical question is whether this represents a new baseline or a temporary spike. Early warning signal for Q1 2026: If January revenue falls back below $350K or burn exceeds $60K/month, December was likely an outlier. If January holds above $400K with sub-$50K burn, the inflection point may be real.
4 Forecasting reliability is compromised—models need recalibration
A 44% revenue miss and 227% EBITDA miss indicate fundamental disconnects between financial models and operational reality. This level of variance suggests the models were built on incorrect assumptions about conversion rates, ARPU, churn, or CAC—not just off by normal estimation error. Strategic implication: Any 2026 forecasts built on the same model structure will be equally unreliable. The company needs a ground-up rebuild of its financial model using actual 2025 cohort data (not assumptions) for CAC by channel, conversion rates by source, monthly ARPU by plan type, and cohort retention curves. Until this is done, board and investors should treat all forward projections as directional only.
5 Operating structure is built for a $6M+ company but only generating $3.5M
Operating costs of $1.57M annually (including $1.04M in salaries) represent a fixed cost base designed for the $6.3M revenue budget, not the $3.5M reality. At current revenue levels, OpEx is 44% of gross sales vs the budgeted 23%. This overhead structure will prevent profitability even if revenue grows 20-30% in 2026. Strategic decision required: Either (1) right-size the team and cost structure to match actual revenue (targeting $1.0-1.2M annual OpEx, requiring ~25-30% cost reduction), or (2) commit to aggressive revenue growth plan to "grow into" the current cost structure (would require reaching $5M+ revenue run rate by end of 2026). The current state—costs for a large company, revenue of a small company—is unsustainable beyond Q3 2026 runway.
